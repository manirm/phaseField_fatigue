Theoretical Manual

The Phase-Field Fatigue Suite is built upon the thermodynamics of variational fracture mechanics. This section details the mathematical models and degradation mechanisms implemented in the engine.

1. Phase-Field Fracture (Variational)

The variational approach to fracture approximates the sharp crack surface \(\Gamma\) with a regularized damage field \(\phi \in [0, 1]\). The total potential energy functional is expressed as:

\[ E(\mathbf{u}, \phi) = \int_{\Omega} \psi(\mathbf{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u}), \phi) \, d\Omega + \int_{\Omega} G_c \gamma(\phi, \nabla \phi) \, d\Omega \]

where \(\psi\) is the elastic energy density and \(\gamma\) is the crack surface density function defined by the Ambrosio-Tortorelli functional:

\[ \gamma(\phi, \nabla \phi) = \frac{1}{2\ell} \phi^2 + \frac{\ell}{2} |\nabla \phi|^2 \]

Energy Splitting (Miehe Model)

To prevent crack propagation under compression, the elastic energy density is decomposed into positive (tensile) and negative (compressive) parts:

\[ \psi(\mathbf{\varepsilon}, \phi) = [g(\phi) + k] \psi_+(\mathbf{\varepsilon}) + \psi_-(\mathbf{\varepsilon}) \]

where \(g(\phi) = (1-\phi)^2\) is the quadratic degradation function and \(k \ll 1\) is a small stability parameter.

2. Environmental Fatigue (Hydrogen)

The suite implements the Hydrogen-Enhanced Decohesion (HEDE) model coupled with stress-assisted mass transport.

Stress-Assisted Diffusion

The transport of hydrogen is driven by both concentration gradients and hydrostatic stress gradients:

\[ \frac{\partial c}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot \left[ D \nabla c - \frac{D c V_H}{R T} \nabla \sigma_h \right] \]

where \(\sigma_h = \text{tr}(\sigma)/3\) is the hydrostatic stress and \(V_H\) is the partial molar volume of hydrogen.

HEDE Mechanism

Local hydrogen occupancy degrades the fracture toughness \(G_c\) through a sensitivity parameter \(\chi\):

\[ G_c(\theta) = G_c(0) \left[ 1 - \chi \theta \right]^2 \]

where \(\theta = c/c_{max}\) is the site occupancy.

3. Fatigue Crack Growth

Cyclic loading is handled via the modified energy density history field \(H\). For Paris' Law integration, the suite extracts the Stress Intensity Factor \(K\) using the numerical J-integral:

\[ J = \int_{A} \left[ \psi n_1 - \sigma_{ij} n_j \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_1} \right] q \, dA \]
Pro Tip For 3D specimens, ensure the J-integral domain (the radius \(q\)) is at least 3-4 times the length scale \(\ell\) for accurate extraction.
